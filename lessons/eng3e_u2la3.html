When considering a persuasive argument, it’s important to recognize the difference between a fact and an opinion.

  • Fact
  • Opinion

A fact is based on evidence. Facts can be proven. “April has 30 days.”

If you count the days on the calendar, you’ll find that April does have 30 days. A fact is true no matter who is experiencing it…

An opinion is a personal view on a topic. “April is the worst month of the year.”

That’s an idea that not everyone will agree with. Therefore, it’s an opinion, but not a fact.

Plan

Follow this next example to help you plan.

The topic is: “Zoos should be banned.”

Strangely, the first thing to think of is why people who hold the opposite argument might have that opinion. Knowing your opponent can help you win the persuasion battle.

Opposing ideas:

  • zoos let people come across animals they would never come across otherwise
  • zoos help people understand and respect wildlife more
  • zoos can encourage people to make donations to wildlife protection organizations.

What if humans were caged?

By Lucinda Zinder

The escape of a monkey from the Animalia Zoo last night is just one more reason why zoos should not exist. Some may argue that zoos are important because they allow people to see wildlife that they otherwise may never get the chance to see. Some will even push the point further by saying that zoos encourage people to respect wildlife and perhaps provide charitable donations to help endangered species. However, last night’s monkey business proves one key point: wild animals do not like to be caged. It is cruel to expect any animal, let alone a wild animal, to spend his or her days in a cage, locked up in an environment that is not its natural habitat, despite efforts to make it as hospitable as possible. Keeping wild animals in a zoo is archaic and the practice should be abolished. The animals would thank you. The money you would normally spend at the zoo could easily be redirected to an organization that protects endangered species. Perhaps then animals like Morris wouldn’t feel the need for a late-night breakout.

Create

Put it together.

Start by stating your topic and your position on the topic. Add your main reason.

Our anti-zoo writer was sparked to write about zoos because of an escaped monkey from the local zoo – this is our “time and place”. Here are the opening sentences:

“The escape of a monkey from the Animalia Zoo last night is just one more reason why zoos should not exist. Last night’s monkey business proves one key point: wild animals do not like to be caged.”

Introduction – Introduce the issue and state the position

The escape of a monkey from the Animalia Zoo last night is just one more reason why zoos should not exist.

Main argument

However, last night’s monkey business proves one key point: wild animals do not like to be caged.

Notebook

Create your opening in your Notebook.

Review the two or three reasons for your position. In what order will they flow best? Rearrange them if you need to, and put them after your opening. For example, the anti-zoo writer goes on:

  • “It is cruel to expect any animal, let alone a wild animal to spend his or her days in a cage, locked up in an environment that is not its natural habitat, despite efforts to make it as hospitable as possible.
  • “Keeping wild animals in a zoo is old-fashioned and the practice should be abolished. The animals would thank you.
  • “The money you would normally spend at the zoo could easily be redirected to an organization that protects endangered species.”

Concluding sentence

Your concluding sentence will restate in other words the main idea the writer wants to convince the reader of.

Consider also that your reader has now read all your points and should not need to be convinced any longer. You are just reminding them of your main point.

Our example concludes:

“Perhaps then, animals like Morris would be free and wouldn’t need a late-night breakout.”

Write your concluding sentence in your Notebook.

Example

Here is an example to help you put all the steps together:

Introduction – Introduce the issue and state the position: The escape of a monkey from the Animalia Zoo last night is just one more reason why zoos should not exist.

Main argument: Last night’s monkey business proves one key point: wild animals do not like to be caged.

Proof – Reason 1: It is cruel to expect any animal, let alone a wild animal, to spend his or her days in a cage, locked up in an environment that is not its natural habitat, despite efforts to make it as hospitable as possible.

Proof – Reason 2: Keeping wild animals in a zoo is archaic and the practice should be abolished. The animals would thank you.

Proof – Reason 3: The money you would normally spend at the zoo could easily be redirected to an organization that protects endangered species.

Conclusion –Perhaps then animals like Morris wouldn’t feel the need for a late-night breakout.
